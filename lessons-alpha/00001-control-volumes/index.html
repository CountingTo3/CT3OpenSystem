Control Volume Analysis

Seen in: Thermodynamics/Fluid Mechanics

ID: 00001   LM: 2025JUN01

 Control volume analysis considers the change in energy of some specific volume. Unlike the experimental idea of "control", the volume can change signifcantly. In this case, "control" refers to the control of your choice of volume.
 For example, a piston from an engine can be considered a control volume despite compressing, thus changing volume, and taking in and exhausting gasoline, thus changing mass.
 According to conservation of energy, the following equation applies.

Ein = Eout

 It is important to note that sign conventions differ. The sign convention I was taught is that heat gain is positive and work done to the environment is positive.

Q = W + E

 For many thermodynamics problems it can be assumed that potential energy does not change. However, there is a possibility that kinetic energy does. Note that the following equations are only true when neither change.

Qin - Win = Qout - Wout

ΔQ = ΔW

 The following is a sample problem:

Problem 1:
Steam enters an adiabatic compressor at 1MPa and 200°C. The steam exits the compressor at 10MPa and 500°C. Determine the work done by the compressor per unit mass in kJ/kg. (h1=2827.4 kJ/kg; h2=3374.0 kJ/kg)

Using the equation ΔQ = ΔW combined with the fact that the compressor is adiabatic, the work done by the compressor is equal to the change in heat of the steam.

Using a steam table (steam tables may vary), h1=2827.4  and  h2=3374.0

Thus, ΔW = ΔQ = Q2 - Q1 = h2 - h1 = 546.6 kJ kg
